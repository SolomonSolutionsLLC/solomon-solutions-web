## TL;DR
- Consulting is simply helping someone solve a problem they care about and getting paid for it. You do not need top credentials, a degree, or 20+ years of experience, just enough know-how to get someone from problem to solution faster, better, or more easily.
- The core building block is the **Offer Formula**: **Skill + Problem + Willing Wallets = Your Offer.** Validate demand with the **10 + 5** method (talk to 10 potential clients, research 5 competitors) before you commit.
- Niche down to an ideal client (but don't over-niche), pick a delivery model (Volume vs. Value; DIY / DWY / DFY; B2B vs. B2C), and price it. Feras lists seven pricing models and recommends beginners start with **hourly or project-based**, then move toward value-based later.
- Your first clients almost always come from your existing network, not ads or fancy websites. Feras gives a direct-outreach playbook: download your LinkedIn contacts, segment into warm/cold, send short non-pitchy messages, follow up, and track everything in a simple CRM.
- Finish with delivery (delight your first clients for references/case studies), back-office setup (entity, bank account, bookkeeping, contracts, insurance, taxes), and using AI as an assistant, not a replacement. The closing message: don't wait to feel ready, act.

### 3. Pillar 2: The Offer Formula (4:53-19:46)
- **Summary:** This is the longest section. The formula is **Skill + Problem + Willing Wallets = Your Offer.** (1) **Skills:** you need relevant, not rare, skills; brainstorm from projects, things others ask your advice on, side work, even hobbies/volunteering (operations, funnels, hiring, sales, taxes, IT, HR). Feras walks through his Skill Assessment Worksheet, scoring hard skills 1-10 and rating soft skills honestly. (2) **Problem/Solution:** "Clients don't pay for skills, they pay for solutions." He gives examples (hiring skills -> "I help companies build reliable hiring systems"; IT infrastructure -> audit/modernize the tech stack; process improvement -> "leverage AI and automation to streamline operations"). An offer is "here's who I help, here's the problem I solve, and here's how I solve it." (3) **Willing Wallets:** validate demand with the **10 + 5** method: talk to 10 potential clients about what they struggle with and what they're unhappy with in current vendors; research 5 competitors (websites, social, reviews) for pricing, gaps, and strengths. He cites Peter Drucker ("My greatest strength as a consultant is to be ignorant and ask a few questions") and a stat that those who identify pain points are 28% more likely to close (Sales Management Association). He closes with a side-hustle vs. all-in discussion (be intentional, block 1-2 hours each evening, bring family into the loop).
- **Key concepts:** Offer Formula (Skill + Problem + Willing Wallets); relevant > rare skills; hard vs. soft skills; outcomes over skills; 10 + 5 validation; identifying underserved demand ("you're not inventing demand"); strategic upskilling/certifications.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Brainstorm every skill, experience, or type of work that could help someone; write it all down (use the Skill Assessment Worksheet, scoring 1-10).
  2. Rate soft skills honestly; flag gaps to learn (e.g., presentation/communication).
  3. For each skill, define the real-world problem it solves and phrase the solution as an offer.
  4. Talk to 10 people who might need it; take notes on their pains, vendor frustrations, and aspirations.
  5. Research 5 competitors' offers, pricing, reviews, content cadence; find a gap to stand out.
  6. Consider a certification/association if it gives a competitive edge.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Run the Offer Formula on each product. Simply Pray's "willing wallets" test is critical since it is pre-launch: do the 10-person interviews with prayer-app users and ministry leaders before scaling spend, and study 5 competitor prayer apps for pricing/gaps (this directly informs the Free/$2.99/$6.99/Church $49 tiers). The Drucker "ask questions" posture fits a trust-driven niche and respects the organic-first, low-budget constraint (interviews cost time, not money).

### 5. Pillar 4: Picking Your Business Model (23:28-26:16)
- **Summary:** Feras breaks down the core delivery decisions. **Volume vs. Value:** volume = lower price, more clients, productized/scalable/automated; value = higher price, fewer clients, custom, deeper relationships (you can blend). **How hands-on:** Advisory/DIY (you guide, they execute), DWY (done with you, collaborative), DFY (done for you, full service), and you can blend per client. **B2B vs. B2C:** B2B = higher contracts, longer sales cycles, ROI-driven decision makers; B2C = faster sales, more emotional buys, lower price points. He advises using your competitor research to decide whether to follow the norm or deliberately disrupt (e.g., if CPAs all charge $1,500 flat for small-business tax prep, you could match it but deliver faster, or switch to hourly).
- **Key concepts:** Volume vs. Value; DIY / DWY / DFY; B2B vs. B2C; following the trend vs. disrupting; packaging the offer.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Choose Volume vs. Value (or a blend) for direction.
  2. Choose how hands-on (DIY/DWY/DFY), possibly per client.
  3. Decide B2B vs. B2C.
  4. Use competitor notes to decide whether to match the market norm or differentiate.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** The ecosystem already maps onto these models. Simply Pray = volume/productized B2C (app tiers) plus a B2B Church tier. HopeStack = value/B2B SaaS. ### 6. Pillar 5: How to Price Your Services (26:16-31:00)
- **Summary:** Feras opens with a story: he told an underpricing language coach to double his rates; the coach feared losing students, raised rates anyway, lost almost none, and doubled income (he notes this worked because the coach was drastically underpricing, and he's not telling everyone to double immediately). Core principle: "clients don't pay for time, they pay for outcomes." He lists **seven pricing models**: Hourly (easy to start, caps growth), Project-based (flat fee for defined scope), Value-based (priced on results, higher fees), Tiered (silver/gold/platinum), Retainer (recurring monthly), Productized (fixed deliverable, fixed price, scalable), and Performance-based (paid on outcomes, lucrative but risky). His guidance for beginners: start with hourly or project-based and document scope carefully. To set an hourly rate, check Indeed, Salary.com, or Glassdoor, or use his formula: **(desired annual income + business expenses) / billable hours**. Example: ($180,000 + $20,000) / 1,600 hours = $125/hour. He personally leans value-based now, but says early on the priority is simply to sell and build experience.
- **Key concepts:** Outcomes over time; seven pricing models; baseline hourly formula; sell first, optimize pricing later; layering models as you grow (DIY course -> DWY group -> DFY high-ticket).
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Pick a starting model: hourly or project-based.
  2. For project-based, document scope thoroughly (what's included and not).
  3. Set an hourly rate from market data (Indeed/Salary.com/Glassdoor) or the formula.
  4. Prioritize landing real clients now; move toward value-based as you gain proof.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Simply Pray's tiers (Free/$2.99/$6.99/Church $49) are exactly the "tiered" and "productized" models Feras describes, so the structure is already sound; the pre-launch job is validating willingness to pay (tie back to the 10-person test). The free 30-min strategy call is the "sell first / build experience" on-ramp. Heartward's $5/$12/$25 tiers also fit the tiered/retainer pattern.
