# How to Start a SaaS Business From Scratch

- **Creator:** Dan Martell
- **URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lxpaSlImFHk
- **Published:** 2024-07-14
- **Duration:** 29:02
- **Source:** Real captions downloaded via yt-dlp and deduplicated (~6,476 words). Section summaries below are grounded in the actual transcript text near each chapter timestamp. Nothing has been invented.

> **Jargon note for Joshua:** "SaaS" = Software as a Service — software people pay for monthly (a subscription) instead of buying once. Simply Pray, HopeStack, and Heartward are all SaaS. Dan's whole pitch is that you "build the software once and get paid every month" (recurring revenue), which is exactly the model your products use.

## TL;DR

- Don't start with a clever idea — start with a real, painful problem a specific customer already has, ideally found through consulting or by watching people's messy spreadsheets and workarounds.
- Before paying anyone to code, build a **clickable prototype** (paper → Balsamiq → Figma → InVision) to test both the *function* (do the APIs/data exist?) and the *flow* (will a user actually understand it?).
- **Validate by getting people to pay you real money up front** (an "early adopter / inner circle" program at ~50% off for a year). If they won't pay, they won't use it. "Nothing survives first contact with the customer."
- Build an **MVP** under a hard 3-month constraint, give your wireframes to developers (never let developers design), and be wary of no-code platforms you don't control.
- Be "maniacal" about customer feedback — call customers, focus on the "yellow" almost-power-users (not the loud "red" non-users), then generate demand (content, paid, partners, press — partnerships are fastest) and find a creative **growth hack** nobody else is using.

### 4. Build a prototype (4:30-8:27)
- **Summary:** Dan's favorite first build is a **clickable prototype / wireframe** — so you don't build something users would never use. His youngest brother had a property-management software idea; instead of coding, they wireframed it with Sharpies and went to a mall asking renters for feedback. They learned tenants didn't want an app — they just wanted to text their landlord — while landlords *did* want to organize and route job orders. Catching that on paper "saved him tens of thousands of dollars." Dan stresses minimizing the information you request from users and getting them "from sign up to value" as fast as possible. A clickable prototype tests two things: **function** (do the APIs / data sources exist to actually solve it — check the documentation of Facebook, Salesforce, a CRM like GoHighLevel) and **flow** (the UX — is it clear?). He lists a prototyping ladder: pen and paper → Balsamiq → Figma → InVision (near pixel-perfect, can install on your phone like working software). Changing a prototype is far cheaper than changing code an engineer wrote.
- **Key concepts:** clickable prototype / wireframe; test before paying engineers; function vs flow; APIs / data sources; UX clarity; time-to-value (signup → value); fidelity ladder (paper → Balsamiq → Figma → InVision).
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Wireframe the solution (start with pen and paper, one sheet per screen/flow).
  2. Lay screens out in the real sequence a user would follow: sign up → enter info → use it → get a report.
  3. Take it to real target users and watch their reactions/questions.
  4. Check **function**: read the API docs of the systems you'd integrate to confirm the data/function is exposed.
  5. Check **flow**: make sure the UX is understandable and minimizes user input ("click click value").
  6. Move up the fidelity ladder (Balsamiq → Figma → InVision) only as needed.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** As a software engineer, Joshua's temptation is to just build — Dan's whole point is *don't*. Before adding Simply Pray features, wireframe the flow and show it to a handful of believers (he has the ministry network, not yet an app audience). Test "function" for Heartward/HeartwardChat now: confirm the AI/theological Q&A API behavior is reliable *and* that the flow is trustworthy/compliant given the faith niche. Minimize signup friction so a praying user reaches value in seconds — critical for a habit app like Simply Pray. AI can draft these wireframes for Joshua to approve, fitting his 2-5 hrs/week.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Balsamiq, Figma, InVision (prototyping tools); Facebook, Salesforce, GoHighLevel (named as examples of APIs/CRMs whose docs you'd check).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Wireframe the Simply Pray core ACTS prayer flow on paper before writing more code.
  - [ ] Show that wireframe to 5 people from your ministry network and write down their questions.
  - [ ] For Heartward, verify both the AI "function" reliability and the trust/clarity of the answer "flow."
  - [ ] Cut every non-essential field between signup and first value.

### 5. Validate (8:27-12:43)
- **Summary:** Step three is to **validate by getting people to pay you money** — Dan rejects "nobody will pay until I build it" by pointing to crowdfunding ($18B/year, where people fund things before they exist). He says a business is "born" the moment someone pays you. People will *lie nicely* and say "great idea, go build it," then not buy — "nothing survives first contact with the customer." His strategy: an **early adopter / "Inner Circle" program** — show the prototype, get feedback, then have them pay up front for a year's license at 50% off, often pitched at the end of a webinar. Perks can include name on the website, a spot on the product roadmap feedback system, or even equity if they have a large audience. He shares that at his company Flowtown they built three fake landing pages and, on the credit-card "thank you" page, didn't actually charge — telling people "servers were over capacity" — just to test willingness to pay. Warning: getting 3 people to pay can be *selling*, not validating. His co-founder called him "Sell Martell" because he'd promise any feature an objector wanted ("yeah we'll build that, give me your money") — which leaves you with 10 different product specs. When you pre-sell, you must keep all 10 customers buying the *same* thing.
- **Key concepts:** validation = real payment; crowdfunding as proof of concept; early adopter / Inner Circle program; 50%-off annual prepay; webinar pre-sell; fake landing-page / payment test; false validation by over-selling; hold one spec across all pre-sales.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Show your prototype and collect feedback.
  2. Invite the best prospects into an early-adopter program.
  3. Get them to pay up front for a year (often ~50% off) — real money.
  4. Offer aligned perks (name on site, roadmap input, sometimes equity for big audiences).
  5. Keep the offered product *identical* across every pre-sale — don't promise a new feature per objection.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** This is the most important step for **pre-launch Simply Pray and HopeStack**. Since Simply Pray isn't on the App Store yet, run a real **waitlist with a paid early-adopter tier** — e.g., founding-member annual access at a discount — to prove people will pay before launch, not just join a free list. For HopeStack (B2B, beta/waitlist), pre-sell annual licenses to a few counseling centers from his consulting network. **Faith/compliance caution:** Dan's "fake landing page / don't actually charge" trick risks eroding trust in a faith niche — Joshua should validate with *honest* prepay/founding-member offers, not a deceptive non-charge. Hold one spec so he doesn't end up custom-building for each church.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** crowdfunding (as an industry/concept); Flowtown (his own past company, as an example).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Replace "free waitlist" with a paid founding-member offer for Simply Pray (honest charge, discounted annual).
  - [ ] Pre-sell HopeStack annual licenses to 2-3 counseling centers before building more.

### 6. Build MVP (12:43-15:59)
- **Summary:** Step four is the **MVP (minimum viable product)** — the key is to **constrain your features**. Dan and his co-founder once had two different product visions; they resolved it by each pre-selling their prototype and seeing who could sell best, under a constraint that features had to be buildable in **3 months max**. Whatever money you raise in validation is your build budget. He warns of a friend who spent **$5 million over 3 years and still hadn't launched** ("you should have called me 3 years ago"); most of his clients launch with paying customers for under $100K, sometimes $50K, by leaning on other software to do heavy lifting. To hire developers: go on **Upwork**, find inexpensive, well-reviewed devs, pay each a small **test project** (one feature) using *your* wireframes — because **developers should not design software** ("developer-designed software usually sucks — ugly and weird"). No-code options (**Bubble, Make.com, GoHighLevel, RapidAPI**) are great for speed, but if it's built on someone else's stack you may have to rewrite it later for an acquirer, and you risk that platform changing pricing/APIs and breaking you (as happened to his Flowtown, which had built on someone else's data/APIs before they had 50,000 customers).
- **Key concepts:** MVP; feature constraint; the 3-month rule; validation money = build budget; Upwork hiring with paid test projects; give devs your wireframes (don't let devs design); no-code trade-offs; platform/dependency risk.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Constrain the MVP to features you can build in 3 months.
  2. Use your validation revenue as the budget.
  3. On Upwork, shortlist well-reviewed, affordable developers.
  4. Pay each a small test project — one feature — built from your wireframes.
  5. Pick the best coder; never hand design to developers.
  6. Consider no-code (Bubble/Make/GoHighLevel/RapidAPI) for speed, but weigh rewrite + platform-dependency risk.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Joshua's 2-5 hrs/week and ~$1,000/mo budget make Dan's constraints a perfect fit — set a 3-month launch box for Simply Pray's MVP. Because Joshua can code, the "don't let devs design" warning becomes "don't let *engineering instincts* sprawl the scope" — wireframe first, then build only the constrained set. **HIPAA caution for HopeStack:** no-code tools that touch protected health information add compliance risk, so the platform-dependency warning is doubly serious there; prefer a stack he controls. Heartward's $5-$25/mo tiers can be validated cheaply before heavy build.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Upwork; Bubble, Make.com, GoHighLevel, RapidAPI (no-code/automation); Flowtown (cautionary example).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Draw a hard line: the Simply Pray MVP must ship within 3 months.
  - [ ] List only the features that fit that 3-month box; park the rest.
  - [ ] For HopeStack, confirm any no-code/3rd-party tool is HIPAA-aligned before using it.
  - [ ] Keep the stack you own for compliance-sensitive products.

### 7. Collect Customer Feedback (15:59-20:30)
- **Summary:** Step five: be **maniacal about getting feedback** — not just watching usage. People lie ("I love that tool") even when they never signed up — false positives. Dan's habit was **"smile and dial"**: 90 minutes every Thursday calling new signups to learn what they thought, how they heard about him, and how to improve. Hiding behind dashboards is "like running a retail store and watching customers on CCTV from the back room instead of talking to them in the store." Always **work backwards from the customer**: ask "what do you do 3 minutes before and 3 minutes after you use our product?" to find expansion opportunities. The wrong question gets the wrong (expensive) solution. Don't just build for the loudest or biggest customer — be an **investigator** across all users. His three-step loop: **collect → analyze (group the problems) → deploy the rollouts**. Crucial targeting: focus on the **"yellow" users** (using it but not power users) — find the 1-2 features that turn them into "green" power users — rather than wasting time on **"red"** non-users. If 100 customers and only 2 are loud about a broken area, working those 2 ignores 98; you could instead get ~40% really using and retaining the product. He plugs his Google Doc of ~100 customer questions: DM "cust Dev" on Instagram (link in description).
- **Key concepts:** maniacal feedback; false positives; "smile and dial"; CCTV/back-room analogy; work backwards from the customer; the "3 minutes before/after" question; ask the right question; red/yellow/green users; collect → analyze → deploy; activation and retention.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Schedule recurring calls with new customers (his "smile and dial," 90 min/week).
  2. Ask what they were trying to accomplish and where they got stuck.
  3. Ask "what do you do 3 minutes before and after using the product?"
  4. Collect all feedback, analyze it into problem groupings, then deploy rollouts.
  5. Target "yellow" almost-power-users; find the 1-2 features that make them "green"; don't over-invest in "red" non-users.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Joshua's pastoral skills are a competitive advantage here — calling Simply Pray early users to ask how their prayer life changed *is* ministry and product research at once. The "3 minutes before/after" question fits a prayer habit perfectly (what triggers them to open the app, what they do after praying). For HopeStack, interview counseling-center staff about where intake/care workflows break. Focus limited hours on "yellow" users — churches trialing Simply Pray's $49/mo plan who haven't fully adopted — and find the one feature that converts them. Use Dan's free 100-question Google Doc (DM "cust Dev").
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Instagram (to DM "cust Dev" for the Google Doc of ~100 customer questions).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Block a weekly 60-90 min "smile and dial" with Simply Pray early users.
  - [ ] Add the "3 minutes before / 3 minutes after" question to every interview.

### 8. Generate Demand (20:30-24:31)
- **Summary:** Step six: there are only **four ways to create demand** — (1) **publish content**, (2) **paid acquisition (ads)**, (3) **partners** (people who already have your customers), and (4) **press**. Dan says startups lean on **partnerships** because they're the fastest way to get in front of a whole audience of perfect-fit customers at once. Pay a partner an **affiliate fee of 20-40% of the customer's monthly lifetime value** so one partner can reach thousands. Example: his client **Matt at Review Wave** worked event stages — a 5-minute pitch sending everyone to his booth, closing customers on-site to fund the travel/sponsorship. Events, webinars, emails, Facebook groups are all places customers aggregate. Paid acquisition means money out first; content/SEO takes a lot of time before the first customer; press (especially podcasts) can work. His core advice: **do the thing you'll keep doing long-term** (blogging, short-form, personal brand). Big warning on **equity**: don't give 10-40% equity to someone who *promises* customers and then doesn't deliver. Instead make them an **affiliate** (e.g., 40%/month of what their audience pays), and *later* let them convert that dollar amount into equity at an agreed valuation. He drives it home: if you sell for $100M years later, you don't want to write a $10M check to someone who got you your first 100 customers who mostly churned — equity is your most valuable asset (for top employees, investors, and your own future shares).
- **Key concepts:** four demand channels (content, paid, partners, press); partnerships are fastest; affiliate fee = 20-40% of monthly LTV; events/webinars/email/Facebook groups as aggregation points; "do what you'll keep doing"; affiliate-before-equity; convert royalties to equity later; protect your equity.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Choose among the four channels — start with partnerships for speed.
  2. Find one partner who already owns thousands of perfect-fit customers.
  3. Pay them an affiliate fee (20-40% of monthly LTV), not equity, at first.
  4. Pick the channel you can sustain long-term and keep doing it.
  5. Only after a partner proves real, recurring revenue, let them convert that dollar amount into equity at an agreed valuation.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** This is gold for someone with **no audience/list/social yet**. Joshua should lean on **partnerships** — denominational networks, ministry leaders, Christian podcasts, and church associations already hold his perfect-fit audience for Simply Pray and Solomon Solutions. Offer an honest affiliate fee rather than equity to a ministry influencer. "Do what you'll keep doing" maps directly to **The Wisdom Walk** (his article/SEO content hub) — the sustainable, organic-first channel that fits his constraints and faith niche. The affiliate-before-equity rule protects him from giving away ownership to a ministry partner who can't actually deliver subscribers.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Review Wave / "Matt" (client example); events, webinars, email, Facebook groups, podcasts (channels, not products).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] List 5 ministry partners/podcasts that already hold your target audience.
  - [ ] Draft an affiliate offer (e.g., a set % of monthly subscription) — not an equity offer.
  - [ ] Commit to one sustainable organic channel (The Wisdom Walk content) and publish on a steady rhythm.
  - [ ] Never give equity to a partner before they prove recurring, paying customers.

### 9. Growth Hack (24:31-29:02 / end)
- **Summary:** Step seven: a **growth hack is not marketing** — it's a *unique customer-acquisition channel most people don't know about*. He tells the WestJet story (they allegedly scraped competitor Air Canada's internal route-demand data via an ex-employee login to decide new routes — got sued, settled; "completely illegal, don't do that") — illustrating the *concept* of finding where demand data lives. Legal examples: at **Clarity**, experts shared slides on **SlideShare**, so his team manually found top experts there, went to the last slide (contact info), and emailed them to join — a feature they called "Bonjour" because it created marketplace liquidity; later automated. For an **HR software in Asia**, they discovered customers using **Google Apps for domains** were likelier to buy, so they ran company domains through a lookup and lead-scored Google-Apps users first — tripling outbound sales productivity. **Uber** entered new cities by putting $20 Uber gift cards in event attendees' gift bags so people would install the app and get home from the event. The recurring questions: **"Who's got my list? Who's got my data? Who's got my customers?"** — who's already done the heavy lifting of finding perfect-fit customers. Another example: a friend paid someone to run ads off *their* Facebook pixel because that audience also bought the friend's product. A growth hack is only a hack if nobody else knows about it and it gives an unfair, unique advantage — it requires creativity, may be hard to scale or feel too complicated for competitors to copy, and you should stay on the legal/ethical edge ("obviously not illegal"). Closing CTA: click the link to learn the best businesses to start in 2024.
- **Key concepts:** growth hack vs marketing; unique/unfair channel; "who's got my list/data/customers"; working backwards from where customers aggregate; lead-scoring on a buying signal; legal/ethical edge; creativity over spend.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Ask: who already has my list / data / customers / has done the heavy lifting?
  2. Find where your perfect-fit customers already aggregate or leave signals.
  3. Reach them directly (manually first), then automate once it works.
  4. Identify a buying signal and prioritize/lead-score on it.
  5. Keep it ethical and legal — creativity, not rule-breaking.
