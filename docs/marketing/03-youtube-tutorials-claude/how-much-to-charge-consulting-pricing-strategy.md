## TL;DR (3-5 bullets)
- **Find a baseline first.** List *everything* you do for a client (onboarding calls, all the back-and-forth emails/texts, plus the actual work), estimate the hours, find a reasonable hourly salary rate (e.g., look up the job title on Glassdoor in a major city), then multiply hours x rate x a margin (he uses 1.3 = a 30% margin) to get a defensible price.
- **There are five ways to actually get paid:** (1) hourly contractor, (2) monthly retainer, (3) flat package, (4) percentage, (5) value-based. He ranks hourly as the worst because going faster means getting paid less.
- **Value-based pricing means charging based on the dollar result you create for the client**, not on how long it takes you. The same service is worth different amounts to a restaurant vs. a car dealership because they make different amounts of money from a new customer.
- **His "3P squared" model stacks offers in sequence:** start with a flat-rate front-end package, add a flat-rate add-on while you work, then transition to an ongoing percentage or retainer once trust is established. Never start with percentage on a brand-new client (you risk doing months of work and not getting paid).
- **The mindset shift:** clients pay for a *result*, not your hours. Once you can articulate the value you deliver, pricing becomes a "no-brainer" for the client.

### 2. Fee Structure / Finding a Baseline (0:27-3:40)
- **Summary:** Jason gives a "quick way to find a baseline." Make a huge list of everything you could do to deliver results for a client, including the first onboarding call, all back-and-forth emails and texts, and the actual work. His example: setting up an ads account for a new marketing agency takes about 30 hours including all communication. Then find a reasonable *salary-equivalent* hourly rate. He explicitly says this is NOT charging per hour; it's only to find a baseline. His method: go to Glassdoor, make a free account, search a job title (he uses "PPC specialist") in a major city (Seattle) so there's no downward cost-of-living adjustment, and see the expected salary (~$80k in his example). Then add a margin because you're a business with overhead (leads, software, tools, laptops), not just an employee. He uses 1.3 (a 30% margin). Math: 30 hours x $50/hr x 1.3 = ~$1,900 to set up a Google Ads account. He also recommends Upwork (set up an account as a *client* to see what freelancers charge for similar services) and Fiverr, but warns NOT to copy their pricing because it's generally low; use them only to understand how to *package* services.
- **Key concepts:** Baseline price; counting ALL time (communication included); salary-equivalent rate (not your charge rate); major-city salary to avoid downward adjustment; business margin/overhead; the formula `hours x hourly rate x margin`.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. List everything you'd do to deliver results (onboarding, all emails/texts, the actual work).
  2. Estimate total hours for that project.
  3. Look up a salary-equivalent hourly rate (Glassdoor, job title, major city).
  4. Pick a margin multiplier (he uses 1.3 for 30%).
  5. Multiply: hours x hourly rate x margin = baseline price.
  6. Cross-check packaging (not pricing) on Upwork/Fiverr.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Run this exact formula for **Solomon Solutions church consulting**. Example a church might recognize: a "communications/ministry strategy" engagement. Count the discovery call, all email back-and-forth, the actual audit/plan, and the review meeting. Find a comparable salary (e.g., "marketing manager" or "communications director" in a major metro on Glassdoor) for a defensible rate, then apply a 30% margin. For **HopeStack** (B2B SaaS, not hourly), this baseline math is less about the subscription and more about pricing *implementation/onboarding services* for a counseling center. For **Simply Pray's Church $49/mo tier**, the baseline thinking helps you sanity-check whether $49 covers your support overhead per church.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Glassdoor, Upwork, Fiverr.

### 7. Value (Value-Based Pricing) (7:55-10:23)
- **Summary:** The model most people have heard about: charge based on the value you deliver. He explains what that actually means with a marketing example across three businesses: a family restaurant, a local tax accountant/bookkeeper, and a car dealership. The value of a new customer differs hugely: ~$25-$125 per family/table for the restaurant, ~$500-$1,500 for the tax accountant, ~$4,500-$12,000 for a used-car dealership. So the *same* service produces very different monetary benefit depending on the client, meaning you can charge the dealership far more than the restaurant for identical work. This influences which clients you want — but he notes everyone chasing "just car dealerships" isn't realistic, so you may end up with one price for restaurants, another for bookkeepers, and a third for big dealerships. Packages can change based on client size. The rule of thumb he gives: charge roughly 10-20% of the value your service delivers (with real math coming in the final section).
- **Key concepts:** Value-based pricing; value of a customer differs by client type; same service, different price; price by client segment; rule of thumb = 10-20% of delivered value.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Identify the dollar value your service creates for that specific client/segment.
  2. Recognize identical work is worth more to higher-value businesses.
  3. Set segment-specific prices/packages.
  4. Aim for ~10-20% of the value delivered.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** This is the **biggest mindset upgrade for Solomon Solutions.** A megachurch and a 60-person church plant get very different *value* from the same communications work, so segment your pricing. For **Simply Pray Church tier ($49/mo)**, frame value in church terms: what is improved prayer engagement / discipleship worth to a congregation? (Be careful and honest — in ministry, lean on concrete, non-manipulative value like staff time saved or members served, not inflated "spiritual ROI" claims.) For **HopeStack**, value-based framing is strong B2B: quantify counselor hours saved, intake throughput, and compliance risk reduced, then price as a fraction of that. The "10-20% of value" heuristic gives you a defensible anchor instead of guessing.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** None.

### 8. 3P (the "3P squared" model) (10:23-15:41)
- **Summary:** Jason shares his own model for stacking pricing tiers so they stay aligned with client goals — he calls it the "3P squared model" (and jokes he should pick a better name; full detail is in his consulting launch playbook). The sequence: (1) **Front-end package** — a flat-rate package for new clients; you onboard, do the work, and off-board by delivering results. (2) **Flat add-on offer** — while doing the work, look for areas to add value and offer another flat-rate service. He warns against saying "sure, no problem" to extra requests, because once you do, "scope creep" floods in with more requests; instead, when out-of-scope asks appear, quote a flat rate (or, if you must, an hourly rate for that one area). (3) **Ongoing service as a percentage** (or a retainer) — after delivering, propose staying on. His agency example: set up the ads account (front-end package), add analytics tracking (flat add-on, because almost nobody has it set up right), then offer ongoing campaign optimization for a percentage of ad spend, OR a retainer for clients who just want access/Q&A rather than active management.

  Then he runs the **final math** comparing all models for the marketing-agency example:
  - **Contractor:** actual hours x hourly rate = 25 hrs x $50 = **$1,200**. Downside: faster work = less pay (he vents about asking for raises after working faster).
  - **Package:** estimated hours x hourly rate x margin = even if it only took 25 hrs, you still get the full **$1,900**. Downside: if it takes 50-60 hrs, it's painful — and that's how you learn to raise prices or avoid the mistake (he says his first client effectively paid him $6-$7/hr due to mistakes).
  - **Retainer:** estimated monthly hours x hourly rate x margin = 10 hrs example = **$650/month** (adjust over time).
  - **Percentage:** client spending $20k ad spend, charge 15% = **$3,000** for the period (a month here).
  - **Value:** car dealership example — getting them 10 extra sales/month at $12,500 = $125,000 in extra sales; at 10% you charge a **$12,000** fee for delivering $125,000 in sales. He notes value is similar to percentage but can be based on cost savings too, calculated however you want. The key: whatever you charge should be in line with what the client ultimately wants, because the client pays for a *result*, not for how long it takes.
- **Key concepts:** The 3P-squared stacked model (front-end package → flat add-on → ongoing percentage/retainer); handling scope creep with flat-rate quotes; result-over-hours mindset; side-by-side math showing package/value beat hourly.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Sell a flat-rate front-end package; onboard, deliver, off-board.
  2. During delivery, identify and offer a flat-rate add-on.
  3. When out-of-scope requests appear, quote a flat rate (don't give free work).
  4. After delivering results, offer ongoing work as a percentage or a retainer.
  5. Run the math across models to confirm package/value out-earn hourly.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Adopt the **3P sequence directly for Solomon Solutions:** Front-end = a flat "Ministry Strategy/Audit" package coming out of your free 30-min call. Add-on = a flat-rate extra discovered mid-engagement (e.g., setting up their email/announcement system, or onboarding them onto a tool). Ongoing = a monthly retainer (better fit than percentage for churches) for continued advisory. This maps perfectly onto your ecosystem: the front-end consulting package can *include or lead into* **Simply Pray (Church tier)** and **HopeStack** as the "ongoing" recurring layer — software becomes the natural retainer. The scope-creep discipline ("quote a flat rate, don't just say yes") protects your 2-5 hrs/week. The final math is your sales argument: show a church a flat package so they get a predictable number and you aren't punished for efficiency.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Consulting launch playbook (for the detailed 3P walkthrough).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Map your offers into the 3P sequence: front-end package → flat add-on → retainer.
  - [ ] Write a one-line script for quoting out-of-scope requests at a flat rate.
  - [ ] Decide where Simply Pray / HopeStack plug in as the "ongoing" recurring layer.
  - [ ] Run the package-vs-hourly math on one real engagement to prove the package wins.
