## TL;DR (3-5 bullets)
- Digital marketing is "just marketing done with digital tools" (SEO, social, paid ads, email, your website) and beats traditional marketing on reach, precision, cost, and especially speed/trackability — but traditional still has its place once you've mastered digital.
- Win with **strategy before tactics**. Adam's "marketing master plan" is five parts in order: **Model, Market, Message, Media, Machine**. The classic beginner mistake is jumping straight to Media (picking a platform) — "shiny object syndrome."
- He frames five "this vs. that" choices: organic vs. paid, direct response vs. brand awareness, search vs. discovery, products vs. services, and B2B vs. B2C. Each is "use the right tool for the right job," not "one is better."
- Strong opinions worth noting: most businesses should **start organic** then scale with paid once they have a proven winner; and "90% of people 90% of the time" should focus **almost exclusively on direct response**, treating brand awareness as a bonus.
- For services specifically: don't sell the service, **sell the transformation** (before vs. after). "Products show features. Services sell feelings, outcomes, and results."

### 2. Digital Marketing vs. Traditional Marketing (0:50–3:14)

### 11. Direct Response Marketing vs. Brand Awareness Marketing (9:46–12:18)
- **Summary:** **Direct response** asks for "a clear action" — buy now, click here, sign up, book a call, download — and the beauty is it's trackable almost instantly (run an ad, get leads; send an email, watch signups). It's "fast, measurable, and ROI-focused." **Brand awareness** is the long game: trust, credibility, top-of-mind, "less buy now, more remember me." Two challenges: you often can't measure it ("you don't get a notification when someone starts trusting your brand"), and it's not as effective at making a sale. The "biggest trap": running a brand awareness campaign but expecting direct response results ("like baking a cake and getting mad when it tastes like bread"). His strong stance: "90% of people 90% of the time" should focus almost exclusively on direct response and treat brand awareness as a bonus.
- **Key concepts:** Direct response (clear CTA, trackable, ROI-focused); brand awareness (trust, top-of-mind, hard to measure, weaker at direct sales); the mismatched-expectations trap; "right tool for the right job"; the 90/90 direct-response recommendation.
- **Step-by-step:** (1) For leads/sales, use direct response with a clear CTA. (2) Track the response (leads, signups, trials). (3) For long-term loyalty, build awareness — but expect awareness-type results, not sales. (4) Default to direct response for ~90% of effort.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Use direct response calls-to-action: Simply Pray "Join the waitlist," Heartward "Start your free questions," consulting "Book your free 30-min strategy call," HopeStack "Request beta access." These are all trackable and budget-friendly. Caution for a trust/compliance niche: keep CTAs honest and low-pressure (no hype) — direct response can still be gentle and pastoral. Treat brand-building (sermons, articles, reputation) as the bonus that rides along, exactly as Adam advises.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Facebook ad, email, YouTube video with a call to action (direct-response examples). No specific tools named.
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Add one clear, honest CTA to every piece of content (waitlist, free call, free trial).
  - [ ] Set up tracking for each CTA (signups, bookings, trials).
  - [ ] Stop expecting sales from pure awareness content; measure awareness differently.

### 12. Search Marketing vs. Discovery Marketing (12:18–14:28)
- **Summary:** The difference comes down to one word: **intent** — why someone is on a platform. **Search marketing** (Google, Bing, Yahoo, YouTube-as-search) reaches people on a mission — they typed "best protein powder" or "how to fix a leaking tap" — so they have intent to buy/learn/do. You reach them via **SEO** (rank organically) or **Google Ads** (pay-per-click for instant top placement): "high intent, high value, converts like crazy." **Discovery marketing** is the "social scroll zone" (Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, YouTube-as-browsing) where people aren't searching — "they're killing time," open to something cool but not typing "best CRM for startups." Your job there is to "stop the scroll," entertain/intrigue/educate, then "lead them somewhere valuable." "Discovery marketing is about earning attention, not capturing intent." Both work; using the wrong approach at the wrong time burns money.
- **Key concepts:** Intent as the dividing line; search = high intent (SEO, Google Ads/PPC); discovery = low intent / scrolling (stop the scroll, earn attention); YouTube is "a bit of both."
- **Step-by-step:** (1) Identify whether your audience has intent (searching) or is browsing (scrolling). (2) For search, win with SEO or PPC. (3) For discovery, stop the scroll with entertaining/intriguing/educational content, then lead somewhere valuable. (4) Match approach to the moment.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Search is Joshua's cheapest, highest-fit channel: people literally search "how to pray," "ACTS prayer method," "Christian counseling near me," "is it a sin to…" — high intent that **The Wisdom Walk** (SEO hub) and **Heartward** can capture organically without ad spend. Use discovery sparingly (he has no audience yet) and only with genuinely valuable, scroll-stopping faith content. For Simply Pray, SEO articles answering prayer questions feed the waitlist funnel.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** Google, Bing, Yahoo, YouTube (search engines); SEO; Google Ads / pay-per-click; Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, YouTube shorts (discovery).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] List the high-intent search phrases your audience types, and write Wisdom Walk articles targeting them (SEO).
  - [ ] Treat any social posting as discovery: lead with a scroll-stopping hook, end with a path to your funnel.
  - [ ] Prioritize search/SEO over discovery given no existing audience and a tight budget.

### 13. Marketing Products vs. Marketing Services (14:28–17:14)
- **Summary:** Most marketing advice was written for **products** (things you can touch, taste, hold) — but **services** are intangible, often paid for upfront before trying, and "a totally different game." Using product tactics for a service "sets you up for frustration." With products you can demo and "highlight features and perks" (he uses a pen: cap = feature, keeps ink off clothes = benefit, clip = convenience, holes in the lid = safety so you don't choke). With services there's "no easy way to make a demo video of a life coach fixing your confidence." The key: "you don't sell the service, you sell the transformation" — the client's life **before** vs. **after**. His business-coach example: client goes from overwhelmed and stuck to having a roadmap, a first hire, and growing revenue after being flat for 2 years. "Products show features. Services sell feelings, outcomes, and results." First shine a light on the pain (to show understanding, not to be a jerk), then paint an irresistible picture of the other side.
- **Key concepts:** Products (tangible, feature/benefit demos) vs. services (intangible, paid upfront); features vs. benefits; sell the transformation (before → after); pain-then-possibility framing; "feelings, outcomes, and results."
- **Step-by-step:** (1) Identify whether you're selling a product or a service. (2) For services, define the client's "before" (pain). (3) Define the "after" (transformation). (4) Market the transformation, not the sessions/service. (5) Show understanding of the pain, then paint the after-picture.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Mixed portfolio: **Simply Pray / Heartward** are app products (can show features — but per Adam, still sell the benefit/feeling, e.g., "a steadier, more consistent prayer life," not just "ACTS templates"). **Consulting and HopeStack onboarding** are services — sell the transformation: church before/after consulting; a counseling center before/after HopeStack (from compliance anxiety + scattered records to organized, HIPAA-aligned care). Lead with the pain pastors/counselors actually feel, then the after-state. This pairs naturally with the "feel understood" message principle (section 6).
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** none (pen used only as a physical illustration).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] For consulting and HopeStack, write the client's before-state (pain) and after-state (transformation).
  - [ ] Rewrite product copy (Simply Pray, Heartward) to lead with the feeling/outcome, not just features.
  - [ ] Draft one "before → after" story per service offering (honest, real clients only).

### 14. B2B Marketing vs B2C Marketing (17:14–19:25)
- **Summary:** **B2B** (business to business) = selling to companies/organizations: fewer customers, bigger deals, longer sales cycles, more decision makers, and it benefits from "logic-heavy pitches" focused on ROI, process, and outcomes. **B2C** (business to consumer) = selling to regular people: simpler, faster buying decisions, benefiting from "more emotion-driven messaging" around desire, identity, and lifestyle. With B2B your audience is small/focused so "every pitch counts"; with B2C you play "a slightly larger volume game." Warning: B2C still doesn't mean targeting "anybody with a pulse" — stay selective. His memorable summary: "B2B buyers want to feel smart and safe, and B2C buyers want to feel seen and excited." The video closes with a pitch for his free digital marketing system / templates / extended HighLevel trial (link in description) and a second video for service-based businesses.
- **Key concepts:** B2B (few customers, big deals, long cycles, multiple decision makers, logic/ROI) vs. B2C (many customers, fast decisions, emotion/identity/lifestyle); "smart and safe" vs. "seen and excited"; B2C still requires targeting.
- **Step-by-step:** (1) Identify whether the buyer is a business or a consumer. (2) For B2B, use logic/ROI/process/outcome messaging to fewer, higher-value buyers. (3) For B2C, use emotion/identity/lifestyle messaging at higher volume. (4) Either way, stay selective about who you target.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:** Joshua spans both. **HopeStack** and **Simply Pray Church ($49/mo)** and **consulting** are **B2B** — counseling centers and churches. Make them "feel smart and safe": lead with ROI, process, and especially the safety angle (HIPAA-alignment, trust, compliance) that fits the niche perfectly. **Simply Pray (individual tiers), Heartward** are **B2C** — individual believers; make them "feel seen and excited" with identity/lifestyle messaging (the kind of prayerful person they want to be), while still targeting the specific avatar from section 5. The closing affiliate pitch is Adam's, not a requirement for you.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** HighLevel / Adam's "digital marketing system" and templates (his own affiliate offer, in description); a linked follow-up video for service-based businesses.
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Split your products into B2B (HopeStack, church plan, consulting) and B2C (Simply Pray individual, Heartward) buckets.
