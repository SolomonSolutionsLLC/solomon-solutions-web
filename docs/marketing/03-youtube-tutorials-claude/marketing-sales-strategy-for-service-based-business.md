## TL;DR (3-5 bullets)

- **Product marketing fails for services because services are invisible** — you're asking someone to buy a promise they can't touch, test, or hold, so the usual product playbook doesn't work.
- **Shift #1 — "Finish Line Language":** Stop selling the process (sessions, deliverables, timelines). Sell the *result* — the better version of the client's life after working with you (more energy, peace of mind, weekends back).
- **Shift #2 — Features vs. Benefits:** A feature is what something *does*; a benefit is what it does *for the client*. Translate every feature into a benefit so the client instantly sees "that's exactly what I need."
- **The CASE Funnel:** A 6-step system (Traffic → Opt-In → Authority Amplifier → Application → Calendar Booking → The Call) that turns strangers into clients without chasing, begging, or pushy tactics. CASE = Coaches, Consultants, Agencies, Service professionals, Experts.
- The video closes with an offer of a free pre-built version of this whole funnel + automations via the creator's affiliate links.

### 2. Shift #1: Finish Line Language — Sell the Result, Not the Process (0:50-2:28)
- **Summary:** Selling a service is selling a promise, and that's the problem: clients can't hold your coaching, feel your design work, or test-drive your consulting, so they must *believe* in a result. When people don't understand the result, they hesitate — which is why providers get stuck describing the process (deliverables, timelines, number of sessions) and miss what the client actually wants. The treadmill example: don't sell incline range, weight capacity, or Bluetooth speakers — show the healthier, stronger, more confident life after using it. "Finish line language" means painting a picture of life *after* working with you, not the steps along the way. Real examples given: a health coach sells "more energy, better sleep, feeling great in your clothes" (not six calls); a marketing agency sells "steady clients, consistent growth, peace of mind" (not content calendars); a landscaper "gives clients their weekends back" (not yard maintenance).
- **Key concepts:** Selling a promise; hesitation from unclear results; "the finish line"; describing the process vs. painting the outcome; making the result "feel undeniably real."
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. Identify the result your client actually wants (the after-state), not your deliverables.
  2. Stop listing process items (sessions, timelines, packages).
  3. Paint a picture of "life after working with you."
  4. Make the result feel real and tangible since the service itself can't be seen.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:**
  - **Simply Pray (priority / pre-launch):** Lead the waitlist page with the finish line — "Pray with focus and consistency again" / "End the day unhurried with God" — not "an app that uses the ACTS method with four tiers."

  - **Heartward:** Sell "answer hard faith questions with confidence" not "AI theological Q&A."
  - **Consulting:** Sell "a clearer path forward for your church" rather than "a free 30-minute strategy call" (keep the call as the mechanism, but the headline is the outcome).
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** none.

### 4. Shift #2: Features vs. Benefits — Make Them Believe It Works (3:07-5:33)
- **Summary:** Once the finish line is clear, the next step is helping people *believe* you can get them there — and this is where many providers go wrong by talking about sessions, tools, and steps without showing why any of it matters (like saying "this coffee cup has a ceramic handle" and expecting "take my money"). Features are what something *does*; benefits are what it does *for the client*. Examples: ceramic handle (feature) → not burning your hand (benefit); pen cap (feature) → no ink stains on your clothes (benefit); baseball stitches (feature) → throwing a perfect curveball (benefit). Service businesses mess this up by marketing "three strategy sessions, a 22-page report, and access to a private Slack channel" — while the client thinks "how does that help me?" The fix: translate every feature into a benefit. ("Three strategy sessions" → "you'll know exactly what to focus on each week"; "22-page report" → "clear roadmaps instead of guessing"; "private Slack" → "support when you need it, not 2 weeks later".) The tent example: the buyer only cares "will this keep me dry in a storm?" Rule of thumb: for every feature you mention, put the benefit right behind it — don't assume people will connect the dots; do it for them.
- **Key concepts:** Feature vs. benefit definition; "why should they care?"; translating features into benefits; the rule of thumb ("for every feature, the benefit is right behind it"); not making people connect the dots themselves.
- **Step-by-step:**
  1. List every feature of your offer (sessions, reports, channels, tools).
  2. For each feature, ask "how does this help the client?"
  3. Rewrite each feature as a benefit (or pair feature + benefit together).
  4. Never leave a feature standing alone — always attach its benefit.
- **Apply to Joshua's products:**
  - **Simply Pray:** "ACTS method (Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication)" is a feature → benefit: "a simple structure so you never run out of words when you pray." "$6.99 tier" feature → benefit: "guided prayer for every part of your day." "Church tier $49/mo" feature → benefit: "your whole congregation praying together, even when apart."
  - **HopeStack:** "HIPAA-aligned" (feature) → "client records that keep you and your counselees protected" (benefit). "Scheduling + notes in one place" → "less admin, more time for actual counseling."
  - **Heartward:** "AI theological Q&A" (feature) → "trustworthy, sourced answers to questions you'd be nervous to ask aloud."
  - Apply the rule of thumb everywhere: pair each spec/tier/feature with its benefit on pricing pages.
- **Tools/resources mentioned:** none (Slack is named only as an illustrative example, not a recommended tool).
- **Action checklist:**
  - [ ] Build a 2-column feature → benefit table for Simply Pray (every tier and feature).
  - [ ] Do the same for HopeStack's beta page.
  - [ ] Edit pricing pages so no feature appears without its benefit beside it.
