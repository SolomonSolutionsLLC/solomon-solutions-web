# Email capture and nurture sequences

> How you turn a stranger who visits your website into someone who hands you their email address, then slowly earns their trust through a planned series of helpful emails.

## Beginner explanation

Let's define the words first, because marketers throw jargon around like it's obvious. It isn't.

**Email list:** Simply the collection of email addresses of people who said "yes, you can email me." It is yours. Unlike followers on a social platform, no algorithm sits between you and them, and no company can take the list away. This is why people say "the money is in the list."

**Opt-in:** The moment a person agrees to join your list. It must be a real "yes" (they typed their email into a box and submitted it), not something you assumed. Two flavors exist:
- **Single opt-in:** They type their email, they're on the list. Fast, but some addresses are fake or mistyped.
- **Double opt-in:** They type their email, then you send one email asking them to click a "confirm" link before they're fully added. Slower, but cleaner. The trade-off: studies suggest 20-30% or more of people never click that confirmation link ([OptinMonster](https://optinmonster.com/double-optin-vs-single-optin-which-one-is-better/)). For a brand-new, trust-sensitive faith audience, double opt-in is the safer default.

**Lead magnet:** A free, genuinely useful resource you give away in exchange for an email address. A PDF, a short guide, a video, a checklist, a mini-course. "Lead" is marketing-speak for a potential customer; the resource is the "magnet" that attracts them. People do not give up their email for "subscribe to my newsletter." They give it up for something specific and valuable.

**Welcome sequence (also called a nurture sequence or drip):** A small set of pre-written emails that automatically go out, one at a time, over the first days after someone joins. "Nurture" because you're growing the relationship, not selling hard. "Drip" because the emails arrive on a slow schedule, like a drip.

**Broadcast vs. automation:** A **broadcast** is a one-time email you write today and blast to your whole list (like a newsletter about this week's article). An **automation** is a sequence set up once that triggers automatically for each new person based on what they did (joining triggers the welcome sequence). You write automations once; they work forever.

**Open rate / click rate:** Two numbers your email tool reports. **Open rate** = the percentage of people who opened the email. **Click rate** (CTR) = the percentage who clicked a link inside. One caveat: Apple's Mail Privacy Protection auto-loads images, which inflates open rates and makes them unreliable. The number marketers now trust most is **click-to-open rate** (of the people who opened, how many clicked) ([MailerLite](https://www.mailerlite.com/blog/compare-your-email-performance-metrics-industry-benchmarks)).

**Deliverability:** Whether your email actually lands in the inbox instead of the spam folder or being rejected entirely. This is a technical setup problem, covered below.

## Why it matters

Three reasons, with facts separated from opinion.

**Fact:** Welcome emails are roughly four times more likely to be opened and five times more likely to generate clicks than ordinary marketing emails ([InboxArmy](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/welcome-email-series/), citing Wordstream). The moment someone joins is the moment they care most. A planned sequence captures that attention; an empty list wastes it.

**Fact:** Non-profit and faith-adjacent senders see some of the highest engagement of any category, around a 54% open rate and a 3.6% click rate, well above e-commerce or retail ([MailerLite](https://www.mailerlite.com/blog/compare-your-email-performance-metrics-industry-benchmarks)). People in this space actually read mission-driven email.

**Opinion (mine, but well-supported):** For a solo founder with no audience yet, email is the single highest-leverage organic channel. You own it, it costs almost nothing to start, and it compounds. Every article on The Wisdom Walk can capture an email; every email can introduce a product. Social posts disappear in hours; an email list is an asset you keep building for years.

## How it applies to YOUR products

You have one ecosystem, "built by a pastor, engineered for the church," and one trust hub: **The Wisdom Walk**. The strategy that fits your $1,000/month, 2-5 hours/week, organic-first, no-audience-yet reality is: **one list, smart tags, content-first.**

Start with a single email list fed by **The Wisdom Walk** articles. Do not build five separate lists for five products; that is more work than you have hours for. Instead, use **tags** (a label the email tool attaches to a contact, like "pastor" or "individual") so you can later send the right message to the right person.

- **The Wisdom Walk** is your lead-magnet engine. Each article ends with a relevant free download. A piece on prayer offers an ACTS prayer guide (feeding **Simply Pray**). A piece on church systems or leadership offers a checklist (feeding **Solomon Solutions** consulting). A piece on counseling care offers a resource for care leaders (feeding **HopeStack**).
- **Simply Pray** is your PRIORITY and is pre-launch. Treat email capture as a **waitlist**. The lead magnet ("Pray with confidence: the ACTS method, a 7-day guide") doubles as both a useful resource and the on-ramp. Tag these people "simply-pray-waitlist" so that when you hit the App Store, one broadcast launches you to a warm audience instead of crickets.
- **HopeStack** (counseling-center CRM, B2B, highest revenue-per-customer) needs a *separate, narrow* nurture path for Care Director Carol. Few leads, but each is worth a lot. A short 3-email sequence pointing toward the beta waitlist and a 1:1 conversation is enough. Because it is HIPAA-aligned, never put real client or patient data in marketing email, and keep claims careful.
- **Solomon Solutions consulting** converts through your founder brand. Your nurture sequence for Pastor Paul should build trust over several emails and then offer the free 30-minute strategy call as the natural next step. High-ticket B2B sells on relationship, which is exactly what email nurture does well.
- **Heartward** (low-price consumer AI Q&A for Devout Dana and Seeker Sam) can be mentioned inside the nurture flow with a soft link; it does not justify its own funnel yet given your hours.

Budget reality: a free email tool covers you for months. You will spend almost nothing here, which leaves the $1,000 for other things.

## Examples

**Lead magnet on The Wisdom Walk:** At the end of an article titled "How to Pray When You Don't Know What to Say," a simple box: "Get the free 7-day ACTS Prayer Guide, the same method inside Simply Pray." Email in, guide delivered, person tagged "prayer / simply-pray-waitlist."

**A 5-email welcome sequence for an individual believer (Devout Dana), drawn from the standard structure ([InboxArmy](https://www.inboxarmy.com/blog/welcome-email-series/)), faith-flavored. Keep each email 150-300 words.**

1. **Immediately, on signup, "Welcome, and here's your guide."** Deliver the promised download. One sentence on who you are: a pastor who also builds software for the church. Set expectations: "You'll hear from me about once a week." Nothing to buy.
2. **Day 2-3, "Why I'm doing this."** Your story. The pastor-engineer mission, why you started Solomon Solutions. This humanizes you and builds the trust your niche runs on.
3. **Day 4-5, "Try this today."** Pure value, no ask. A short teaching on the ACTS prayer method they can use right now. This is the email that earns the right to everything later.
4. **Day 7-8, "You're not the only one."** Social proof and gentle direction: a short testimony, a line about Simply Pray launching soon, an invitation to join the waitlist if they haven't.
5. **Day 10-11, "Where to next."** Point to your best free resources, invite a reply ("hit reply and tell me what you're praying about") because replies boost deliverability, and softly name the products that fit them.

**Broadcast example:** Every Friday, a short email linking your newest Wisdom Walk article. Written once that week, sent to everyone. This is the habit that keeps the list warm between launches.

## Common mistakes

- **Asking for the email with nothing in return.** "Sign up for updates" converts poorly (average opt-in around 2%, top marketers near 6.5%, per [Sumo via mycodelesswebsite](https://mycodelesswebsite.com/lead-magnet-statistics/)). A specific lead magnet is the fix.
- **Selling in email one.** In a trust-driven faith audience this backfires fast. Give value for several emails before any ask.
- **Skipping the technical setup (SPF, DKIM, DMARC).** These are DNS records that prove your email is really from you. Without them your mail lands in spam or gets rejected; Gmail and Yahoo tightened enforcement through 2025 ([PowerDMARC](https://powerdmarc.com/google-and-yahoo-email-authentication-requirements/)). Your email tool walks you through this; do it once on solomonsolutions.tech before sending.
- **Treating open rate as truth.** Apple inflates it. Watch click-to-open and replies instead.
- **Buying or scraping email lists.** Never. It is illegal under CAN-SPAM norms, destroys deliverability, and is wrong for a ministry. Only email people who said yes.
- **No unsubscribe link, or a hidden one.** Required by law, and keeping your spam-complaint rate under 0.1% protects your sender reputation ([PowerDMARC](https://powerdmarc.com/google-and-yahoo-email-authentication-requirements/)).
- **Over-promising compliance for HopeStack.** Marketing email is not a HIPAA-safe channel for client data. Keep it generic.
- **Building five funnels with five hours a week.** One list, tags, one strong sequence. Expand later.
