# Heartward — Funnel & Growth Plan

> The trustworthy AI study partner for Reformed believers and honest seekers: ask any theology or Bible question and get a warm, Scripture-rooted answer cited from 200+ Puritan and Reformed works — built by a pastor, not a vendor.

---

**How to read this plan (for a marketing beginner).** This document is a step-by-step growth playbook for one product: Heartward (the consumer app at heartwardchat.com). A few terms used throughout, defined in plain English:

- **Funnel** — the path a stranger takes from "never heard of you" to "paying customer." Top of funnel = awareness, middle = trust-building, bottom = the sale.
- **Lead magnet** — a free, valuable thing (a PDF, a tool) you give away in exchange for someone's email address. It "magnetizes" leads (potential customers) to you.
- **Organic** — traffic and attention you earn without paying for ads (search results, articles, social posts, word of mouth). The opposite is **paid** (you buy clicks).
- **SEO** — Search Engine Optimization. Writing pages so Google ranks them when people search.
- **AI search / GEO (Generative Engine Optimization)** — getting your content cited *inside* AI answers (Google's AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity), not just in the blue links.
- **CTA** — Call to Action. The button or line that tells the reader what to do next ("Start your free trial").
- **ARPU** — Average Revenue Per User. Heartward's is low ($5–$25/mo), which is why this plan leans on free, scalable organic channels instead of expensive ads.
- **Conversion rate** — the percentage of people who take the next step (e.g., visitors who start a trial; trials that become paid).

The whole plan respects your real constraints: ~$1,000/mo budget, 2–5 hours/week, organic-first, no email list or social following yet (clean slate), and a faith niche where trust is everything and manipulation is forbidden.

## Primary audience

**Devout Dana — the committed Reformed believer who studies.**

Dana is a 28–55-year-old Christian in the Reformed tradition (Presbyterian, Reformed Baptist, confessional, or "young, restless, Reformed"). She listens to sermon podcasts, owns a stack of books she hasn't finished, and genuinely wants to go deeper — but she's busy. When a question hits her at 10pm ("What did the Puritans actually mean by *assurance*?" / "Is the covenant of works in Scripture or just in the confessions?"), her options today are bad: a generic AI that invents fake citations and theological mush, a 40-tab Google rabbit hole, or a $400 Logos library she half-knows how to use.

**Why Dana is the primary target:**
- She already trusts the *tradition* Heartward is built on, so the product's Reformed depth is a feature, not a niche limitation — it's the entire reason she'd pick it over ChatGPT.
- She is the most likely to convert a free trial to a paid plan because the value ("answers I can actually trust, with sources I can verify") is immediate and obvious to her.
- She refers. Reformed believers travel in tight, high-trust communities (small groups, Reformed Twitter/X, Reddit's r/Reformed, church book clubs). One delighted Dana brings five more.
- Low ARPU demands volume; Dana is the persona most willing to pay *and* advocate, which is how a $5–$25/mo product survives.

**Her core fear / objection:** "Will this AI make stuff up or feed me theology that's off?" Trust and citation-integrity are the whole sale.

## Secondary audience

**Seeker Sam — the honest questioner outside (or on the edge of) the faith.**

Sam is 22–40, deconstructing or reconstructing, curious but burned. He's asking real questions — "Why does a good God allow suffering?", "Is the resurrection historically credible?", "What do Christians actually believe about hell?" — and he wants a thoughtful, non-defensive answer he can explore privately, without a pushy human on the other end or judgment in the room.

**Why Sam is secondary, not primary:**
- Higher volume of search demand (existential questions are searched constantly), but lower trust in the *brand* and lower willingness to pay up front.
- He's a slower, gentler conversion. He converts to *reading*, then to *trial*, then maybe to *paid* — over weeks, not minutes.
- He's served by the same engine and much of the same content, so he's nearly free to reach. The "Privacy Protected — your spiritual journey is sacred" promise on the live site is built for Sam.

Treat Sam as the wide top of the funnel that feeds awareness, SEO, and goodwill; treat Dana as the part of the funnel that pays the bills.

## Lead magnet ideas (3, ranked, with why each works)

A lead magnet's job here is to (1) collect an email so you can nurture trust over time (religion has the highest email open rates of any industry — see Conversion metrics), and (2) demonstrate the exact thing the product does best: trustworthy, cited, Reformed answers.

**1. "10 Hard Questions About the Faith — Answered from Scripture and the Puritans" (PDF / email mini-course).** *Ranked #1.*
A short, beautifully typeset guide that takes 10 real questions (5 for Dana — assurance, covenant theology, the right use of the law; 5 for Sam — suffering, hell, the reliability of Scripture) and answers each in 150–250 words *with numbered citations to the actual works in Heartward's library.*
**Why it works:** It is a free, downloadable demo of the product's core promise — depth + receipts. It serves both personas in one asset. It doubles as SEO/AI-search content (publish each answer as a web page too). It's cheap and on-brand (a pastor sharing wisdom, not a vendor pitching). The citations *are* the conversion mechanism: "I want this for *my* questions" → start the trial.

**2. "The Reformed Reader's Cheat Sheet: 200+ Puritan & Reformed Works, Sorted by What They Help You With" (resource library / printable).** *Ranked #2 — best for Dana specifically.*
A curated, categorized index of the works in Heartward's corpus (Assurance → Brooks, Affliction → Watson, Holiness → Owen, etc.), framed as "where to start with the Puritans without drowning."
**Why it works:** It's pure value to a serious reader, establishes the breadth of the library (the moat) before any sale, and is intensely shareable inside Reformed communities. It signals "we've actually read this stuff," which builds the credibility a skeptical Dana needs. Low effort to produce (you have the corpus list already).

**3. "Is This AI Safe for My Faith? A Pastor's Honest Guide to Using AI for Bible Study (and where it lies to you)." *Ranked #3 — the trust play.*
A candid explainer of why generic AI hallucinates citations, mangles theology, and why a sourced, tradition-grounded tool is different — written in your pastor-not-vendor voice, naming the real risks honestly.
**Why it works:** It meets the #1 objection head-on (and pastors/believers are adopting AI fast but nervously, so the search demand is rising). It earns trust by being honest about AI's failures *including the temptation to oversell your own*. Risk: it's more "category education" than "product demo," so it converts slower than #1 — hence the ranking. Excellent top-of-funnel for Sam and for pastors who'd later champion the tool.

## Best channels (1–2, decisive, with why — respect 2–5 hrs/week)

**Pick exactly two. Do them well. Ignore everything else for 90 days.**

**Channel 1 (primary): SEO + AI-search content, published through The Wisdom Walk and Heartward's own blog/FAQ.**
This is the right primary channel because:
- Heartward's moat *is* its answers. Publishing genuinely excellent, cited answers to the exact questions Dana and Sam already type into Google and ChatGPT turns your core competency directly into distribution. The product and the marketing are the same activity.
- It compounds. An article ranks (or gets AI-cited) for months/years with no ongoing spend — essential for a $5–$25/mo product that can't afford to buy every customer.
- It's the *only* channel that scales to the volume a low-ARPU product needs, on a $0 marginal cost.
- Google's AI Overviews are eating organic clicks, so the goal isn't just ranking — it's being the *cited source inside the AI answer* (see AI search strategy). Your Reformed depth makes you uniquely citable.
- Fits 2–5 hrs/week: AI drafts each answer-article from your corpus; you (pastor) edit for theological accuracy and voice, then publish. One polished piece/week is enough.

**Channel 2 (secondary): Email — the relationship and conversion engine.**
- Religion has the **highest email open rates of any industry — ~55–60%** ([MailerLite / industry benchmarks](https://www.mailerlite.com/blog/compare-your-email-performance-metrics-industry-benchmarks); [Prospeo industry data](https://prospeo.io/s/average-email-open-rate-by-industry)). For a trust-driven faith product, this is a gift you'd be foolish to ignore.
- The lead magnet captures emails from your SEO traffic; the nurture sequence turns readers into trial users into subscribers. Email is where the *sale* happens; SEO is where the *attention* is earned.
- Nearly free (MailerLite free tier) and very low time cost once the welcome sequence is written (write it once, it runs forever).

**Why NOT social-first or paid-first:** You have no following (social is a slow grind for little near-term return), faith content gets throttled on paid ad platforms (so paid is both expensive *and* risky here), and your hours are scarce. Social plays a *supporting* role (repurpose your articles — see Social section), not a lead role. Paid waits until organic proves a message (see Paid section).

**The engine:** one excellent, cited "answer article" per week. Each one is simultaneously (a) SEO content, (b) AI-search bait, (c) raw material for 2–3 social posts, and (d) proof of the product's value. Publish on **The Wisdom Walk** (your article hub, for breadth/trust) and/or the Heartward blog (for product-intent topics), cross-linking to a Heartward trial CTA.

**Four content themes (rotate weekly):**

1. **"Hard questions, honestly answered" (serves Sam + reaches new audiences).** Suffering, doubt, hell, the reliability of Scripture, science and faith. High search volume, gentle on-ramp. Always end with: "Have a follow-up question? Heartward will answer it from Scripture and the Reformed tradition — free for 7 days."

2. **"Going deeper in the Reformed tradition" (serves Dana — your buyer).** Assurance, covenant theology, the Puritans on affliction, sanctification, the right use of the law. Lower volume, *much* higher intent. These articles convert.

3. **"The Puritans on ___ " (showcases the moat).** Mine the corpus: "What Thomas Watson taught about contentment," "John Owen on killing sin." Each one proves the library is real and deep, and is naturally cited-friendly for AI search.

4. **"AI + faith, used wisely" (serves nervous adopters, incl. pastors who become champions).** "Can Christians trust AI for Bible study?", "Why AI invents fake Bible citations (and how to spot it)." Rides the fast pastoral AI-adoption wave; positions Heartward as the trustworthy option.

**Cadence (realistic for 2–5 hrs/week):**
- **Weekly:** 1 answer-article (AI drafts from corpus → you edit/approve → publish with FAQ schema). ~90–120 min.
- **2–3x/week:** repurpose that article into social posts (~20 min total, batch on one day).
- **Weekly:** 1 email to your growing list (often the article + a personal pastoral note). ~20 min.
- **Monthly:** refresh one older article (AI search rewards content updated in the last 6–12 months — see AI section). ~30 min.

**Wisdom Walk synergy:** The Wisdom Walk is your trust-and-traffic hub for the broader audience; Heartward is the tool the articles point to. Theme 1 and 4 articles live naturally on The Wisdom Walk (wider net, less salesy), with a soft Heartward CTA; Themes 2 and 3 can live closer to Heartward (higher intent). One library of work, two front doors.

## Paid ad strategy (only if/when appropriate; if not yet, say so + the trigger to start)

**Not yet. Do not spend on ads in the first 90 days.** Reasons:
1. **Organic must prove a message first.** You don't yet know which headline/angle converts. Paying to amplify an unproven message wastes scarce dollars.
2. **Faith content is throttled on the major ad platforms.** Religious targeting and creative routinely get restricted or disapproved on Meta and Google, so faith advertisers fight an uphill battle — bad odds for a beginner on a $1,000/mo budget.
3. **Low ARPU + cooling trial conversions.** Free-trial conversion has fallen industry-wide (from ~46% to ~33% YoY per [Recurly's 2025 data, via Adapty](https://adapty.io/blog/trial-conversion-rates-for-in-app-subscriptions/)). Paying to acquire $5–$12/mo users before your funnel converts well is a money-loser.

**The trigger to start paid (all three must be true):**
- You have a landing page headline + lead magnet that *organically* convert at or above the benchmarks in Conversion metrics (proven message), AND
- Your welcome email sequence reliably turns leads into trials into paid subscribers (proven funnel), AND
- You know your rough **LTV** (lifetime value — total revenue an average subscriber brings before they cancel) and it comfortably exceeds your expected cost to acquire a customer.

**When triggered, start tiny and safe:**
- Budget: $150–$250/mo of your $1,000, no more, to start.
- **Best paid bet for this niche: search/intent, not interruption.** Google Search ads on high-intent, *non-restricted* queries (e.g., "AI Bible study tool," "theology question answered") tend to clear policy better than faith-*interest* social targeting, and they catch people already looking.
- Promote the *lead magnet* (low friction, list-building) before promoting the trial directly.
- Consider niche faith newsletter sponsorships and Reformed podcast read-spots — often cheaper, warmer, and policy-safe compared to platform ads.

## AI search optimization strategy (how to be the cited answer for this product's questions)

The shift that matters: Google's AI Overviews and ChatGPT/Perplexity increasingly *answer* the question on-screen, cutting clicks to your site. The win is no longer just ranking — it's being **the source the AI cites.** Heartward's Reformed depth + real citations make it unusually citable. Tactics, drawn from 2026 GEO research:

1. **Answer in the first 100 words.** Open every article and FAQ with a self-contained, 40–60-word direct answer to the question. ~44% of ChatGPT citations come from the first 30% of the page ([Contently, 2026](https://contently.com/2026/02/25/how-to-get-cited-google-ai-overviews/); [Averi GEO playbook](https://www.averi.ai/blog/google-ai-overviews-optimization-how-to-get-featured-in-2026)).

2. **Use definitive, confident language.** Cited text is nearly 2x more likely to make clear claims vs. hedged, qualifier-heavy writing (Contently). State what the Reformed tradition teaches plainly, then nuance. (This also fits the brand's *confident* voice.)

3. **Add structured data (schema).** Mark up pages with **FAQPage, Article, and Organization** JSON-LD. FAQ schema correlates with ~40% higher citation weighting in ChatGPT; structured data can lift AI visibility ~30% ([Averi](https://www.averi.ai/blog/google-ai-overviews-optimization-how-to-get-featured-in-2026)). This is a one-time technical setup you (engineer) can do.

4. **Phrase H2s as the user's actual question.** "Does AI make up Bible verses?" beats "AI Accuracy Concerns." AI engines match question-shaped headings.

5. **Cite primary sources by name.** Name the works and authors (Owen, Watson, Westminster Confession) explicitly. Original, named, verifiable references earn 30–40% higher AI visibility ([Contently]). Your corpus is a citation goldmine — use it openly in the public content, not just inside the app.

6. **Keep content fresh.** 53% of AI-cited content was updated within 6 months; pages updated within 12 months are ~2x more likely to be cited ([Contently]). Hence the monthly refresh in the content cadence.

7. **Let AI crawlers in.** Ensure your robots.txt allows GPTBot, Google-Extended, PerplexityBot, etc. (engineer task — verify once).

8. **Build entity association.** Repeatedly link "Heartward" with "Reformed," "Puritan," "cited theology answers" across your pages so engines learn what Heartward *is* and surface it for those queries.

## Conversion metrics (the 3–5 numbers to watch + rough target benchmarks)

Track these in GA4 + Search Console + MailerLite (all free). Don't over-measure — these five tell the story.

1. **Landing-page → trial-start rate.** % of LP visitors who start the free trial. *Rough target:* 2–5% early, push toward 5%+ as copy improves. (This is your headline test's scoreboard.)

2. **Visitor → email signup rate (lead magnet).** % of content visitors who give an email. *Rough target:* 1–3% of cold traffic; 20–40% on a dedicated lead-magnet page.

3. **Email open rate.** *Benchmark:* religion runs the **highest of any industry, ~55–60%** ([MailerLite](https://www.mailerlite.com/blog/compare-your-email-performance-metrics-industry-benchmarks); [Prospeo](https://prospeo.io/s/average-email-open-rate-by-industry)). If you're below ~45%, your subject lines or list quality need work. (Note: Apple Mail Privacy inflates opens ~15–20 pts, so trust *trends* over absolute numbers.)

4. **Trial → paid conversion rate.** The number that decides whether the business works. *Benchmark:* median trial-to-paid is ~38%, but **opt-in / no-credit-card trials (yours) convert far lower — roughly 8–9%** vs. 30%+ for card-required trials ([Adapty 2026](https://adapty.io/blog/trial-conversion-rates-for-in-app-subscriptions/); [RevenueCat State of Subscription Apps 2025](https://www.revenuecat.com/state-of-subscription-apps-2025/)). *Realistic target:* 8–15% to start; improving onboarding/nurture pushes it up. (If you later require a card, expect more conversion but far fewer trials — test deliberately.)

5. **Monthly churn rate.** % of paying subscribers who cancel each month. *Rough target:* keep under ~5–8%/mo; lower is the difference between growth and a leaky bucket at this ARPU.

*(Bonus to glance at, not obsess over: organic impressions/clicks in Search Console — your leading indicator that the SEO engine is catching.)*

## Compliance / brand guardrails (what NOT to say for this product)

This is a faith product in a trust-driven, manipulation-averse niche, and an AI product making truth claims. Guard both.

**Never do:**
- **No guilt or fear-based selling.** No "a real Christian would…," no spiritual shaming, no "you're failing God without this." The brand serves; it never coerces.
- **No fake scarcity or false urgency.** No fake countdowns, "only 3 spots left," or invented deadlines. Trust is the asset; one manipulative tactic burns it.
- **No overclaiming the AI's authority.** Heartward is a *study companion*, **not** Scripture, not the Holy Spirit, not a substitute for the local church, pastoral care, or your own reading of the Bible. Say this plainly. Never imply the AI is infallible or divinely inspired.
- **No claiming answers are perfect.** It's in active development and AI can err — be honest. Position citations as *the user's tool to verify*, and encourage them to check the sources. (Honesty here is also your differentiator.)
- **Don't mix the donation ask with the purchase decision.** Keep "Support the Mission" tiers on a separate page; never leverage mission/guilt to drive product sales.
- **No theological overreach beyond the tradition.** Don't present contested in-house Reformed debates as settled, and be clear the tool reflects the **Reformed/Puritan tradition** specifically — don't imply it speaks for all of Christianity.
- **Privacy is sacred — and it's a promise you must keep.** You market "we never share or sell your conversations." Make sure the product and policy actually honor that; never market a privacy claim you can't back.
- **Mind ad-platform faith restrictions.** If/when you advertise, expect religious targeting/creative scrutiny; keep claims clean and avoid sensitive-category targeting that triggers disapprovals.

**Always do:** lead with honesty, cite sources, point people *back* to Scripture and the church, and let the depth of the work do the persuading. "Built by a pastor, not a vendor" is a standard to live up to, not just a tagline.

## If Heartward is actually a B2B library/CMS platform

The owner originally described Heartward as a *library/CMS with semantic search + a chatbot* — i.e., infrastructure a church, seminary, or publisher could license to make *their* corpus searchable and answerable, rather than a consumer Q&A app. The live site markets the consumer app, so this plan targets that. **If the real product is the B2B platform, the funnel shifts substantially:**

- **Audience flips to organizations.** Primary buyers become **seminaries, Bible colleges, denominations, and Christian publishers** (who own large text corpora and want them searchable/monetizable), plus **large churches** wanting a branded study tool. Personas shift toward **Pastor Paul** (champion/decision-influencer) and seminary librarians/academic deans/publishing-ops leads (economic buyers). Devout Dana becomes the *end user* of a client's deployment, not your customer.

- **Offer flips from $5/mo subscriptions to platform deals.** Pricing becomes seats/usage/annual licenses (think hundreds to thousands of dollars/yr per institution), often with setup/onboarding fees and a custom-corpus ingestion service. ARPU goes way up; volume needed goes way down. The "volume via organic" logic relaxes.

- **Channel flips from broad SEO to targeted outreach + proof.** With few, high-value buyers, the lead engine becomes: a sharp **case study / demo** (ingest a prospect's corpus, show cited semantic answers), **direct/warm outreach** to seminaries and publishers, **conference presence** (ETS, denominational meetings, Christian publishing events), and **founder-led sales** — not a self-serve trial. Email nurture becomes a longer, consultative B2B sequence aimed at booking a demo, not starting a trial.

- **Lead magnets flip to B2B assets.** Instead of "10 Hard Questions," lead with **"Make Your Library Answer Questions: a guide for seminaries & publishers,"** an **ROI/one-pager**, and a **live demo on the prospect's own content.**

- **Metrics flip.** Watch **demos booked, pilot-to-contract rate, average contract value, and sales-cycle length** instead of trial→paid % and consumer churn.

- **What stays the same:** the moat (Reformed/Puritan depth + real citations), the brand voice ("built by a pastor, not a vendor" plays *even better* B2B), the no-manipulation guardrails, the AI-search/SEO content as top-of-funnel credibility, and email as the relationship engine. **Recommendation:** confirm the actual product with the owner before committing budget — the consumer and B2B funnels diverge enough that running both at once would exceed a 2–5 hr/week, $1,000/mo constraint. If both are real, the B2B platform (high ARPU, fewer customers) is the better fit for a solo founder with scarce hours; the consumer app is better suited once there's a team to feed the volume engine.
