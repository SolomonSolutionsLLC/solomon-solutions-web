# Should Your Church Use AI? 7 Honest Questions to Ask First

*By Joshua Kirk — pastor and software engineer*

**The short answer: yes, your church can use AI, but only after you answer a few honest questions first.** AI is a tool, like a printing press or a projector — useful in the right hands, dangerous when it does work that only a person, or the Spirit, should do. The question is not whether AI is coming to your church. It already has. The question is whether you will use it wisely, on your terms, or let it use you on someone else's.

I write this as a pastor who also builds software. I have stood in a pulpit on Sunday and debugged a server on Monday. So I am not here to sell you a tool or to scare you away from one. I am here to hand you the seven questions I would ask before I let any AI near a congregation I was responsible for.

Let's walk through them.

## 1. Where does our data go when we use AI?

This is the first question because it is the one most pastors skip, and the one that does the most quiet damage.

When a staff member pastes a counseling note, a prayer request, or a benevolence case into a public AI tool, that text leaves your church. It travels to a company's servers. Depending on the settings, it may be stored, reviewed, or used to train the next version of the model. You have just handed someone's confessed sin or medical crisis to a third party your congregant never agreed to trust.

Scripture takes confidentiality seriously. "Whoever goes about slandering reveals secrets, but he who is trustworthy in spirit keeps a thing covered" (Proverbs 11:13). A church runs on trust. People tell their pastor things they tell no one else. Before you use AI for anything pastoral, you need a plain answer to one question: where does this data go, and who can see it?

There is a better way than the public tools. A church can run its own private AI, a model hosted on infrastructure the church controls, so staff get the help without sending anyone's private words out the door. More on that under question four.

## 2. Can we trust what it tells us about Scripture and doctrine?

A general-purpose AI is confident, fluent, and often wrong about theology. It will invent a Bible verse that does not exist. It will attribute a quote to Spurgeon that Spurgeon never said. It will smooth four contradictory traditions into one bland paragraph that belongs to no church at all.

This is not a small flaw. A tool that sounds authoritative and cites fake sources is more dangerous than one that admits it does not know. "The one who states his case first seems right, until the other comes and examines him" (Proverbs 18:17). AI states its case first, every time, and it sounds right.

So the rule is simple. Never let an AI be your final authority on a matter of doctrine. Use it to draft, to summarize, to surface a passage you had forgotten — then check the actual text, the actual sources, the actual confession. If a tool cannot show you its sources so you can verify them yourself, treat its theology as a rumor, not a ruling.

This is exactly why I helped build **Heartward**, a Scripture-rooted AI study companion for conservative-evangelical and Reformed believers. Every answer it gives is cited from real Puritan and Reformed works, with numbered sources you can check. The citations are the point. A general AI guesses about your faith; a tool worth trusting shows its work.

## 3. Will this assist our people, or quietly replace them?

Here is the question that keeps me up at night, and it has nothing to do with technology. It is a question about discipleship.

AI is very good at producing words. It can write a devotional, draft a small-group lesson, answer a member's late-night question, and compose a condolence note. The danger is not that it does these things badly. The danger is that it does them well enough that we stop doing them ourselves — and the doing was never the point. The relationship was.

When a grieving widow gets a card, what matters is not the prose. It is that her pastor sat down and thought about her. When a new believer asks a hard question, the answer matters, but so does the older saint who walks alongside her while she wrestles with it. Ministry is not the efficient delivery of religious content. It is people, given to people, in the name of Christ. "We were ready to share with you not only the gospel of God but also our own selves, because you had become very dear to us" (1 Thessalonians 2:8).

Use AI to clear the desk so you have more time for people. Never use it to avoid the people. The test for any tool: does this give me more of myself to give away, or does it let me give less of myself and feel fine about it?

## 4. Who controls the model — us, or a company we have never met?

Most churches using AI today are renting it. The model lives on a corporation's servers. The terms can change. The price can change. The data policy can change. And every prompt your staff sends is a small dependency on a company whose interests are not your congregation's interests.

For a lot of ordinary tasks, renting is fine. You do not need to own the electric company to turn on a light. But ministry involves things you would not want to rent: confidential care, sensitive records, the private words of people who trusted you with them. For that, control matters.

This is where my engineering side and my pastoral side meet. It is now genuinely possible to run a capable AI privately: a large language model deployed on a church's own server, where the data never leaves your control, the policy is one you set, and no one else is reading over your shoulder. It costs more effort to set up than typing into a free website, and it is worth it for the work that touches people's lives. "The prudent sees danger and hides himself, but the simple go on and suffer for it" (Proverbs 22:3). Owning the tool, for the sensitive work, is the prudent move.

## 5. Are we being honest with our congregation about how we use it?

If you used AI to help write Sunday's sermon, would you be comfortable telling the congregation? Sit with that for a moment, because your gut reaction is the real answer to whether you should.

There is nothing inherently wrong with using a tool to organize your study, check your exegesis, or tighten an outline. Pastors have used commentaries, study Bibles, and other people's sermons for centuries. But there is a line between using a tool to sharpen your own work and outsourcing the work entirely while letting people believe it was yours. One is stewardship. The other is a quiet dishonesty that will eventually cost you the pulpit's trust.

"Therefore, having put away falsehood, let each one of you speak the truth with his neighbor, for we are members one of another" (Ephesians 4:25). You do not need to footnote every tool you touch. But you do need to be the genuine author of what you preach, and you should never let a machine impersonate your voice or your care. A simple gut check: if explaining your AI use to your elders would embarrass you, change the use, not the explanation.

## 6. Does this serve the mission, or just make us feel modern?

Churches waste enormous amounts of money and attention chasing tools they do not need. AI is the newest temptation in a long line. It is easy to adopt something because it is impressive, or because the church down the road is using it, or because not using it feels like falling behind.

But novelty is not a ministry strategy. Before you bring in any AI, ask what it is actually for. Does it free your staff from drudgery so they can shepherd people? Does it help a tired volunteer do a real job faster? Does it remove a barrier between a seeker and a clear answer? Good. If instead it is a solution looking for a problem, leave it on the shelf. "So teach us to number our days that we may get a heart of wisdom" (Psalm 90:12). Your days and your church's dollars are finite. Spend them on what makes disciples, not on what makes you look current.

The right question is never "can we use AI here?" It is "should we, and does it serve the mission we were actually given?"

## 7. Who is responsible when it gets something wrong?

AI does not bear responsibility. You do. When a tool gives a bad answer, mishandles a record, or produces something hurtful, "the computer did it" is not a defense a shepherd gets to use. "Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers" (Acts 20:28). The oversight is yours. It cannot be delegated to a machine.

So before you deploy anything, decide who reviews what. A human reads what AI drafts before it reaches a member. A human checks any theology before it is taught. A human owns the privacy decisions. Name that person. Write down the guardrails. AI in ministry should always have a person standing between the tool and the people it touches — not as a formality, but because accountability is part of the job God gave you.

## The honest bottom line

AI can be a real help to a church — clearing busywork, sharpening study, answering questions faithfully, giving overwhelmed staff their time back for the people in front of them. It can also leak your congregation's secrets, teach them error with a confident voice, and slowly replace the human ministry that was the whole point. The difference is not the technology. It is the wisdom of the hands holding it.

That is the work, and you do not have to figure it out alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it ethical for churches to use AI?**
Yes, AI use can be fully ethical for a church when it assists people rather than replacing them, protects congregants' private data, stays under human oversight, and is never passed off as something more than a tool. The ethics live in how you use it, not in the technology itself. Run it through the seven questions above before you deploy anything.

**Should pastors use AI to write sermons?**
A pastor can use AI to organize study, check exegesis, or tighten an outline, the same way pastors have long used commentaries and study Bibles. The line is authorship and honesty: you must be the genuine author of what you preach, and you should never let a tool impersonate your voice or do the spiritual work in your place. A good gut check is whether you would be comfortable telling your congregation how you used it.

**How do we keep our church's data private when using AI?**
Public AI tools may store or review what you send them, so never paste confidential counseling notes, prayer requests, or member records into a free public model. For sensitive work, use a private AI — a model hosted on infrastructure your church controls — so the data never leaves your hands. At minimum, read the data policy of any tool before staff use it for ministry.

**Can AI be trusted for Bible study and theology?**
A general-purpose AI is fluent but unreliable on Scripture and doctrine; it can invent verses and fabricate citations. Never treat it as a final authority. Use tools that show their sources so you can verify them yourself, like Heartward, which cites real Reformed and Puritan works for every answer, and always check the actual biblical text and your confession.

**Will AI replace pastors or church staff?**
It should not, and a healthy church will not let it. AI can handle words and tasks, but ministry is people given to people in the name of Christ, and that cannot be automated. The right use of AI frees staff from drudgery so they have more of themselves to give to the people in front of them, not less.
